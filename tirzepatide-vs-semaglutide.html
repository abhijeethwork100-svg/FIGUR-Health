Why does tirzepatide produce more weight loss?

The GIP advantage

The addition of GIP receptor agonism is the key driver of tirzepatide's superior efficacy. GIP acts on adipose (fat) tissue to regulate fat storage and lipolysis. In the presence of a GLP-1 background, GIP receptor activation appears to shift the body's energy balance more aggressively toward fat oxidation.[1]

Interestingly, research by Finan et al. (2016) showed that GIP receptor agonism alone does not produce meaningful weight loss â€” but when combined with GLP-1 agonism, the two receptors produce a synergistic, supra-additive response that exceeds what either hormone can achieve alone.[5]

Greater appetite suppression

Tirzepatide appears to suppress appetite more powerfully. In a sub-study of SURMOUNT-1, participants on tirzepatide reported significantly greater reductions in hunger, food cravings, and prospective food consumption compared to historical semaglutide data â€” consistent with its dual-receptor action in the hypothalamus.[2]

Improved insulin sensitivity and fat metabolism

Beyond appetite, tirzepatide produces greater improvements in insulin sensitivity, fasting glucose, triglycerides, and liver fat than semaglutide in comparative analyses. This suggests a broader metabolic benefit that may contribute to its weight-loss advantage over time.[6]

Are the side effects different?

Both drugs share a very similar gastrointestinal side-effect profile, reflecting their shared GLP-1 mechanism. The most common adverse events reported in trials were:

  • Nausea â€” most common early on, typically peaks in the first 4â€“8 weeks
  • Vomiting â€” occurs in a minority; usually transient
  • Diarrhoea and constipation â€” can alternate; dietary fibre and hydration help
  • Injection site reactions â€” mild redness or bruising at the injection site

The rates of adverse events leading to discontinuation were virtually identical in the two pivotal trials: 6.9% for tirzepatide (SURMOUNT-1) vs 7.0% for semaglutide (STEP-1). Neither drug has been shown to cause significantly more gastrointestinal distress than the other at equivalent tolerability thresholds.[2,3]

Rare but serious risks â€” the same for both

Both drugs carry class warnings for:

  • Possible risk of medullary thyroid carcinoma (in patients with personal/family history of MEN2)
  • Pancreatitis (rare; patients with a history of pancreatitis should discuss with their doctor)
  • Gallbladder disease (increased rate of gallstones with rapid weight loss)
  • Acute kidney injury (rare; associated with severe vomiting/dehydration)

Neither drug should be used during pregnancy. Medical supervision throughout treatment is essential.

Who should take which drug?

Choose tirzepatide (Mounjaro) if:

  • Your primary goal is maximum weight loss
  • You have type 2 diabetes (tirzepatide produces superior HbA1c reduction)
  • You have not achieved adequate results with semaglutide
  • You want the drug with the most robust clinical trial evidence for â‰¥20% weight loss

Choose semaglutide (Wegovy) if:

  • You have established cardiovascular disease â€” the SELECT trial demonstrated a 20% reduction in major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) with semaglutide 2.4 mg, making it the first obesity medication with proven CV benefit[7]
  • Tirzepatide is not accessible in your region or country
  • You have previously tolerated semaglutide well and are seeing results
Neither drug is a one-size-fits-all solution. Your doctor will consider your full medical history, comorbidities, weight loss targets, and individual response before recommending a treatment.
