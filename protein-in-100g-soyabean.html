FAQs

  • How much protein do I need per day?

    The recommended daily protein intake varies by age, gender, and activity level.

    For maintenance purposes, adults need about 0.8–1g of protein per kg of body weight. So for a 60kg person, that's 48–60g daily.

    However, if you're active, trying to lose weight, or build muscle, you may need 1.2–2g of protein per kg of body weight.

    Eating healthy protein sources like soya can help you meet a significant portion of this requirement. 100g of boiled soya provides 18g protein, which is about 30–40% of most people's daily needs.

  • Does soya cause weight gain?

    No, soya itself doesn't cause weight gain. Like any food, how much you weigh depends on your eating habits and calorie intake. In fact, soya is relatively low in calories while being high in protein. This way, it keeps you full longer.

    100g of boiled soya has only 172 calories, which makes it an excellent choice for weight management (when eaten in moderation).

  • Can I eat soya every day?

    Yes, eating soya daily is safe for most people. Studies suggest that consuming 25–30g of soya protein daily (equivalent to 2–3 servings) can provide a variety of health benefits without side effects.

    This could be a cup of soy milk with breakfast, tofu bhurji for lunch, or soya chunks in your dinner. Try to include soya in different forms in your diet.

  • Is soya suitable for people with diabetes?

    Yes, soya is actually beneficial for people with diabetes.

    It has a low glycaemic index of 15, which means it doesn't cause sharp spikes in blood sugar levels.

    The protein and isoflavones in soya can also improve insulin sensitivity and keep you away from unwanted food cravings.

    However, you must consult your doctor about adding soya to your meal plan.

  • What's the difference between soya chunks and soybeans?

    Soybeans are the whole bean in its natural form (raw or boiled).

    Soya chunks, on the other hand, are made from high-protein, low-fat soy flour by removing oil from soybeans and then shaped into chunks.

    Protein content:

    • Soya chunks (dry): ~52g protein per 100g
    • Boiled soybeans: ~18g protein per 100g

    Both soybeans and soy chunks are nutritious options, but soy chunks have a higher protein concentration.

  • Can men eat soya? Does it affect testosterone?

    Yes, men can safely eat soya.

    There’s a huge myth that soya messes with men’s hormones, but studies show this isn’t true.

    The isoflavones in soya are very mild, and when you eat soya in normal portions, they don’t lower testosterone.

  • How much soya is safe to eat in a day?

    Most people can safely consume 25–30g of soya protein daily, which equals about 2–3 servings. For context, this could be:

    • 1 cup (200ml) of soya milk + 50g of cooked soya chunks
    • 150g of tofu + 1 cup of soya milk
    • 1 bowl of soya chunk sabzi + 2 soya flour chapatis.

    This amount provides good protein benefits while staying well within the safe limits established by health authorities worldwide.

  • Can eating too much soya cause side effects?

    Eating excessive amounts of soya (significantly more than 50g daily) may cause digestive discomfort like bloating or gas, especially if your body isn't used to it.

    In rare cases, excessive intake may affect the absorption of minerals such as iron and zinc.

    To avoid side effects:

    • Start with small portions if you're new to soya.
    • Drink plenty of water, as soya is high in fibre.
    • Limit yourself to eating 25–30g of soya protein per day.

    If you experience persistent digestive issues, reduce your intake or consult a healthcare professional.

  • Is soya suitable for children and teenagers?

    Yes, soya is safe and nutritious for children and teenagers when consumed in age-appropriate amounts. It provides them with protein, calcium, and iron, which are essential for the healthy development of the brain and body.

    Choose whole soya foods, such as milk, tofu, or boiled soybeans, rather than processed soya supplements.

    If your child has any allergies or health conditions, consult a paediatrician before introducing soya regularly.

  • Is soya gluten-free?

    Yes, soya is naturally gluten-free. Pure soya products like soybeans, tofu, soya milk, and edamame contain no gluten. This makes them safe for people with coeliac disease or gluten sensitivity.

    However, be cautious with:

    • Soya sauce: Traditional soya sauce contains wheat and is NOT gluten-free. Look for tamari or gluten-free soya sauce alternatives.
    • Processed soya products: Some soya chunks, soya-based snacks, or meat substitutes may contain wheat or gluten as fillers.

    Tip: Look for "certified gluten-free" labels when buying packaged soya products.

    Many product labels also indicate whether the food item was processed in a facility that also processes allergens such as peanuts, tree nuts, soya, wheat, or dairy.

  • Can pregnant or breastfeeding women eat soya?

    Yes, it is safe for pregnant or breastfeeding women to eat soya in moderate amounts.

    It provides protein, calcium, and folate. But women with thyroid issues should talk to their doctor before adding large amounts of soya into their diet.
